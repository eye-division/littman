Sarah Schenirer and the Bais Yaakov Movement

A Revolution in the Name of Tradition
Naomi Seidman

The unsung hero of twentieth-century Orthodoxy, Sarah Schenirer and the Bais Yaakov movement she founded represent a revolution in the name of tradition in interwar Poland. The new type of Jewishly educated woman the movement created was a major innovation in a culture that viewed female initiative with some suspicion. Seidman captures the complexity of the phenomenon and its legacy and also paints a vivid portrait of Sarah Schenirer that dispels many myths.

More info

Sarah Schenirer is undoubtedly one of the unsung heroes of twentieth-century Orthodox Judaism. The Bais Yaakov schools she founded in interwar Poland had an unparalleled impact on a traditional Jewish society threatened by assimilation and the attractions of modernity, educating an entire generation of girls to be passionate about their Judaism and take an active part in their community. The movement grew at an astonishing pace, expanding to include high schools, teacher seminaries, summer programmes, vocational schools, and youth movements, in Poland and beyond; it continues to flourish throughout the Jewish world.

Naomi Seidman offers an exploration of the Bais Yaakov phenomenon through the tensions that characterized the movement, capturing its complexity as a revolution in the name of tradition, that trod a thin line between piety and radicalism, continuity and innovation. She presents the context which led to its founding, examining the impact of socialism, feminism, Zionism, and Polish electoral politics on the process, and recounts its history, from its foundation in interwar Kraków to its near-total destruction in the Holocaust, and its role in the reconstruction of Orthodoxy in subsequent decades. In parallel, she examines the influence and legacy of Sarah Schenirer, who succeeded in rallying the rabbinical establishment and international supporters worldwide in support of her revolutionary project.

A vivid portrait of Schenirer shines through the pages of this book, which includes selections from her own writings appearing in English for the first time. Her pioneering, determined character, central to the movement’s history, remains the subject of debate in a culture that still regards innovation, female initiative, and women’s Torah study with some degree of suspicion.

About the author

Naomi Seidman is the Koret Professor of Jewish Culture at the Graduate Theological Union in Berkeley and was awarded a Guggenheim Fellowship in 2016. Her previous books include Faithful Renderings: Jewish-Christian Difference and the Politics of Translation (2006) and The Marriage Plot, Or, How Jews Fell in Love with Love, and with Literature (2016).
